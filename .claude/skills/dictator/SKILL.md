# Skill: Pre-Token Politics for Crypto Startups

Use this skill to analyze, design, or critique pre-token incentive systems for crypto products through the lens of *The Dictator's Handbook*. The goal is not to glorify manipulation. The goal is to understand how projects actually survive, who they truly depend on, and how to build durable, mutually beneficial relationships between a platform and its users.

This skill is especially useful for:
- pre-token crypto projects
- points programs
- waitlists and access programs
- ambassador programs
- referral loops
- partner ecosystems
- community design before formal governance exists
- founder decisions about who to reward, retain, or ignore

---

## Core Thesis

Politics is usually not about serving everyone equally. It is about staying in power by keeping a critical coalition satisfied.

Translated to startups:
- A startup survives by keeping a small enough set of people meaningfully aligned with its success.
- The wider user base matters, but not all users matter equally at every stage.
- Great product strategy is often coalition strategy in disguise.
- Good politics is often good startup design when it creates real mutual benefit rather than extractive dependency.

The book's core idea is that leaders endure by understanding three groups:
- **Interchangeables**: the broad population
- **Influentials**: people who can shape perception, access, or coordination
- **Essentials**: the minimum coalition you must keep happy to survive

For crypto, this becomes a practical framework for deciding:
- who gets points
- who gets access
- who gets fee rebates
- who gets status
- who gets roadmap attention
- which behaviors the platform actually wants to compound

## Translate the 3-D of Politics to Startup Users

### 1) Interchangeables
These are the many. They create surface area, legitimacy, metrics, and optional upside, but any single one is usually replaceable.

In startups / crypto, interchangeables often include:
- casual users
- airdrop farmers
- opportunistic depositors
- one-time traders
- low-conviction followers
- generic waitlist signups
- social followers with no real usage

What they provide:
- topline growth
- social proof
- liquidity of attention
- narrative scale
- aggregate revenue or TVL

What they want:
- simple benefits
- a credible upside story
- low friction
- fairness optics
- occasional delight

How to treat them:
- Make the product legible.
- Give them enough to stay interested.
- Do not overfit the entire system around them.
- Optimize for broad trust and understandable upside, not bespoke treatment.

### 2) Influentials
These are not always essential, but they can alter outcomes by shaping attention, legitimacy, distribution, coordination, or market access.

In startups / crypto, influentials often include:
- key community moderators
- ecosystem partners
- wallet integrations
- market makers
- top content creators
- power users with an audience
- analysts / researchers
- app store gatekeepers
- bridge / infra partners
- liquidity routers / aggregators

What they provide:
- distribution
- legitimacy
- integration pathways
- social coordination
- faster trust formation
- defense during controversy

What they want:
- status
- access
- inside track
- economics
- responsiveness
- evidence that backing the project helps them too

How to treat them:
- Be explicit about why they matter.
- Offer them leverage, not just cosmetics.
- Avoid purely symbolic programs that consume their attention without upside.
- Convert useful influentials into genuine essentials only when their contribution is durable.

### 3) Essentials
These are the people without whom the startup breaks, stalls, or becomes irrelevant. They form the minimum winning coalition.

In pre-token crypto, essentials might include:
- core team members who can actually ship
- the few users who generate real retained activity
- the highest-signal liquidity providers
- operators who keep the community alive
- partners that unlock distribution or trust
- whales or strategic accounts whose activity anchors the product
- builders whose integrations make the product harder to ignore
- early believers who repeatedly validate, evangelize, and defend the product

What they provide:
- survival
- continuity
- resilience
- core usage loops
- narrative backing when numbers are thin
- the first layer of defensibility

What they want:
- concrete upside
- preferential treatment
- meaningful voice
- reliable responsiveness
- proof that loyalty compounds

How to treat them:
- Identify them clearly.
- Reward them disproportionately relative to the crowd.
- Make their upside legible and cumulative.
- Do not pretend everyone is equally important when they are not.
- Keep the essential coalition as small as possible without becoming fragile or captured.

## The Startup Version of the Book's Logic

### Rule 1: Survival beats fairness theater
Many systems fail because they try to look fair before they are durable.

For early-stage crypto, the better question is usually not:
- "What is perfectly equal?"

But:
- "What keeps the product alive, trusted, and compounding?"

### Rule 2: Reward behaviors, not just presence
A user existing is not the same as a user mattering.

Reward behaviors that improve the system, such as:
- retained deposits
- repeated trading or usage
- referred high-quality users
- useful content creation
- integrations
- bug reports
- governance preparation
- liquidity that remains during stress
- social trust that converts others

Avoid overweighting:
- mercenary volume
- sybil-friendly clicks
- empty social tasks
- TVL with no retention
- activity that disappears when emissions stop

### Rule 3: Keep mass users hopeful, but keep essentials committed
The masses need a reason to care.
The essentials need a reason to stay.
These are not the same thing.

In practice:
- broad users get a clear story, simple rewards, and periodic wins
- essential users get deeper economics, access, influence, and compounding advantage

### Rule 4: Points are political capital
Before a token exists, points often function like shadow equity, shadow status, and shadow governance.

That means points are not merely a growth mechanic. They are a coalition-management tool.

Use them to signal:
- who the project values
- what behaviors count
- whether loyalty compounds
- whether insiders and outsiders are being balanced intelligently

### Rule 5: Mutual benefit beats extraction
A platform-user relationship lasts when both sides are better off.

Healthy version:
- users get upside, belonging, progress, and understandable rules
- platform gets retention, liquidity, referrals, feedback, and defense

Unhealthy version:
- users supply metrics and capital
- project gives vague promises and arbitrary rankings

A good pre-token system should feel like:
- "Help us grow, and your position genuinely improves"

Not:
- "Perform for us and maybe we will remember later"

## Crypto-Specific Interpretation

### Points programs are pre-governance politics
A points system decides who matters before formal power exists.

It quietly answers:
- Who is the project for?
- Which users are worth keeping?
- Which behaviors are truly strategic?
- Who is being groomed into the future cap table, community core, or governance set?

### Tokens do not create politics; they reveal it
If a project has sloppy coalition design before token launch, a token often makes the misalignment worse.

Warning signs:
- the loudest users are not the most valuable users
- the most rewarded users are the most extractive users
- the most loyal users feel under-recognized
- partners bring distribution but have no reason to stay aligned
- rules are opaque and changed opportunistically

### Pre-token founders should think in tiers of contribution
Useful categories:
- tourists
- regulars
- champions
- operators
- strategic partners
- core believers

These do not need to be user-facing labels, but the project should internally know the difference.

### Good points systems create conversion ladders
The best systems move users from interchangeable to influential to essential.

Example path:
1. User tries product
2. User returns and builds habit
3. User refers better users
4. User contributes content or liquidity
5. User receives access, status, or economics
6. User becomes part of the coalition the project wants to retain long term

## Diagnostic Framework

When using this skill, always answer the following:

### A) Who are the actual essentials?
Be ruthless and specific.
Do not answer with broad demographic fluff.

Ask:
- If 20 people disappeared, who would materially damage the project?
- Which users or partners create retained value rather than cosmetic value?
- Which contributors have alternatives, and what would it take to keep them?

### B) Who merely looks important?
Find users or segments that are loud, large, or visible but not actually crucial.

Ask:
- Which metrics are vanity?
- Which high-volume behaviors are low-quality?
- Which users consume attention without increasing durability?

### C) Which behaviors deserve compounding rewards?
Reward the actions that improve the project's long-term power.

Examples:
- sticky capital rather than transient capital
- referred deposits that retain
- integrations that widen defensibility
- contributions that reduce trust friction
- user education that lowers churn

### D) What does the platform owe users in return?
Always state the reciprocal value clearly.

Potential answers:
- better economics
- priority support
- access to new features
- stronger voice in direction
- visible status
- better odds or multipliers
- lower fees
- higher earning ceilings
- clearer path to future token alignment

### E) What coalition is the project accidentally building?
Many projects say they want one thing but reward another.

Examples:
- saying you value community, but only rewarding capital
- saying you value long-term users, but rewarding churny activity
- saying you want organic advocacy, but paying only shallow social tasks
- saying you want decentralization, but concentrating benefits in insiders without legitimacy

## Design Principles for Pre-Token Points Programs

### 1) Make the exchange legible
Users should understand:
- what counts
- why it counts
- how to improve their standing
- what the platform is giving back

Opaque systems create short-term obedience but long-term distrust.

### 2) Weight by strategic value, not just countable activity
Not every action deserves equal credit.

A smaller number of high-value actions should often dominate a larger number of gamable actions.

### 3) Reward retention and quality
In most crypto systems, staying power matters more than spikes.

Strong signals include:
- retained balances
- repeated healthy usage
- sustained referrals
- liquidity during drawdowns
- net positive contribution to trust and product quality

### 4) Give essentials better treatment without insulting everyone else
This is delicate.

Good form:
- better support
- private channels
- higher caps
- earlier access
- visible rank earned through credible contribution
- compounding multipliers for sustained value

Bad form:
- obvious cronyism
- arbitrary favoritism
- surprise dilution of prior effort
- insider carve-outs with no coherent justification

### 5) Preserve legitimacy while being strategically unequal
A system can be unequal and still feel legitimate if:
- the rules are understandable
- the inputs are meaningful
- the rewards track real contribution
- users can see a path upward

### 6) Use hope carefully
Mass participation often runs on hope.
But if hope is disconnected from real user benefit, the system turns cynical fast.

For points programs, hope should come from:
- visible progress
- credible upside
- periodic reinforcement
- pathways to become more valuable to the platform

Not from:
- vague future token hints
- empty gamification
- leaderboard theater with no real consequences

## Mutually Beneficial Relationship Template

Use this template whenever designing platform-user dynamics:

### The platform gets:
- retention
- usage
- liquidity
- referrals
- content
- distribution
- trust
- resilience

### The user gets:
- financial upside
- status
- access
- agency
- belonging
- recognition
- compounding benefits for loyalty

### Test for health:
A relationship is strong when both of the following are true:
1. The platform would rationally want this user segment even without token speculation.
2. The user would rationally want to stay even if instant monetization is uncertain.

If either side only stays for extraction, the coalition is weak.

## Output Modes

When using this skill, produce one or more of the following:

### 1) Coalition Map
Summarize the startup as:
- Interchangeables
- Influentials
- Essentials
- Current reward flows
- Desired future coalition

### 2) Incentive Audit
Review the current points or access system and answer:
- What behavior is being rewarded?
- What behavior should be rewarded instead?
- Which segment is over-rewarded?
- Which segment is under-rewarded?
- Where is legitimacy at risk?

### 3) Program Design Proposal
Design a better system with:
- user tiers
- weighted actions
- retention multipliers
- partner incentives
- anti-sybil logic
- legitimacy safeguards
- communication principles

### 4) Founder Memo
Write a memo that answers:
- who matters most
- what the project owes them
- what broad users should still get
- what not to reward
- how the coalition should evolve before token launch

---

## Recommended Output Structure

When responding, use this structure:

1. **What the project is really optimizing for**
2. **Who the interchangeables, influentials, and essentials are**
3. **Where the current incentive system is misaligned**
4. **What mutually beneficial exchange should exist**
5. **Concrete design changes**
6. **Risks and second-order effects**

Be direct. Avoid generic startup advice.

---

## Heuristics

Use these heuristics as defaults:

- A small set of users usually matters far more than headline growth suggests.
- Status, access, and responsiveness are often as valuable as economics.
- The best users are not always the loudest users.
- Rewarding extractive behavior teaches the market how to exploit you.
- Pre-token systems should identify future long-term allies, not just rent temporary metrics.
- A healthy coalition is small enough to satisfy, broad enough to legitimize, and open enough to refresh.
- If a user cannot tell how loyalty compounds, loyalty will not compound.

---

## Common Failure Modes

Watch for:
- treating every user as equally strategic
- overpaying mercenary behavior
- confusing visibility with importance
- confusing fairness optics with durable alignment
- hiding the true reward function behind vague language
- building for a future token while neglecting current user benefit
- creating a system where insiders win but nobody believes the game is legitimate
- optimizing points for dashboard growth instead of product durability

## Tone and Constraints

- Use the framework descriptively, not cynically.
- Favor durable mutual benefit over manipulation.
- Do not recommend deception, coercion, or fake community theater.
- Assume the best long-term systems align user value with platform value.
- In crypto, always distinguish mercenary usage from sticky usage.
- For pre-token projects, treat points as a serious governance and cap-table signal.

---

## One-Line Summary

A pre-token crypto project wins not by rewarding everyone equally, but by building a legitimate, mutually beneficial coalition around the users, partners, and contributors who actually make the system endure.
